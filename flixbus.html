FlixBus Seat Selection & Trip Results

Role UX / UI Design, User Research, Interaction Design
Duration 2 Weeks · 2025
Tools Figma, Google Docs

Context

FlixBus is an intercity bus service that lets riders search routes, compare prices, and book tickets online. In class, my team evaluated the current site with usability testing to understand how well the booking flow supports real travelers.


Across five tasks—booking a one-way trip, comparing routes, understanding stops, tracking a trip, and modifying a booking—we noticed repeated confusion around trip results, stop information, and the seat selection / extras flow. The experience often felt cluttered, unpredictable, and easy to misinterpret, especially when users expected an airline-style seat picker.

The Problem

Travelers struggled to understand their routes and seats. Many users missed the seat selection step entirely or couldn’t tell which seats were available, premium, or already taken. Premium fees only appeared on hover, making price changes feel like a surprise at checkout. At the same time, trip results showed dense lists of routes with unclear “direct” labels and scattered stop details, forcing users to dig for basic information like how many stops there were and where they would actually board.

The Solution

We redesigned two key parts of the flow: the trip results / route understanding component and the seat selection & extras experience. A consolidated trip card makes stops, duration, and “no stops vs. number of stops” immediately visible. A dedicated “Seat Selection & Extras (Optional)” step comes before checkout, with a clear legend, high-contrast seat states, transparent pricing, and a prominent option to skip extras altogether. Finally, a review page summarizes seat selection in plain language, giving users one last chance to confirm or change their choice.

Seat Selection Flow

These three screens show the optional seat selection experience from start → selected seat → add-on confirmation. The legend is separated from the seat map, seat states are clearer, and the “Selected Seats” summary updates immediately to reduce surprises at checkout.



Redesign Highlights

  • Transparent route cards: consolidated trip information with a vertical stop timeline, clear “No stops / 4 stops” labels, and more visible map access so users immediately understand how their trip is structured.


  • Specific stop context: improved copy for boarding locations and map pins that can be tied to landmarks, helping riders feel confident they’ve picked the right station.


  • Dedicated “Seat Selection & Extras” step: seat selection and add-ons are grouped into a single page before checkout, with a bold “Optional” label and a prominent “Skip and continue to checkout” button.


  • High-contrast seat legend: available, premium, taken, and selected seats use distinct colors and iconography, with the legend detached from the map so users can actually notice and read it. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}


  • Predictable pricing: selecting a seat updates the right-hand summary tile with seat type and cost—no more hidden hover tooltips or surprise fees on the last screen.


  • Review page confirmation: a dedicated Seat Selection tile on the review screen spells out the current state (“No seat selected yet” vs. “You’ve reserved a back aisle seat”) and offers clear actions to change or cancel before paying.

Final Takeaways

This project reinforced how small pieces of ambiguity around stops, seat states, or pricing—can snowball into a booking flow that feels stressful and untrustworthy.

Group complex decisions into clear steps

When key actions like seat selection and add-ons are buried inside checkout, users feel blindsided. Moving them to a dedicated, clearly labeled step made the flow more predictable and aligned with mental models from airline sites.

Make “optional” truly feel optional

Explicit labels, skip buttons, and calm language go a long way in building trust. Labeling seat selection and upgrades as optional reduced pressure and helped riders feel in control instead of upsold.

Use review screens to lower anxiety

A well-designed review page is not just a recap—it’s a reassurance step. Showing seat status, route details, and next steps in plain language gave users confidence that they understood exactly what they were about to purchase.

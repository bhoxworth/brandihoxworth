Sephora Product Page Redesign

Reimagining Sephora’s product details page (PDP) to feel less like a cluttered website on a phone—and more like a calm, confidence-building shopping experience.

Role

UX Designer · Researcher

Team

3 designers · COGS 187A

Timeline

4 weeks · 2025

Overview

We redesigned the Sephora iOS product details page to keep its premium brand feel while fixing usability issues that made shopping stressful: tiny text, cluttered layout, and confusing delivery and size options.

Through two rounds of user testing (8 participants total), we focused on helping shoppers quickly understand if a product fits their skin needs, compare options with less effort, and feel confident adding items to their bag.

The Problem

  • Cluttered, overwhelming layout – users described the PDP as “too much stuff on the screen” and “like a website slapped onto a phone.”
  • Hard to compare products – shoppers had to switch between multiple tabs and re-read long descriptions just to compare moisturizers.
  • Confusing fulfillment options – pickup & delivery choices were wordy and stacked, making it hard to see what was actually selected.
  • Poor information hierarchy – important details like skin type suitability, ingredients, and “clean” badges were buried in dense text.

Design Goal

Make the PDP feel clear and supportive for all types of shoppers— from casual gift-givers to skincare nerds—without losing Sephora’s luxury aesthetic.

Research & Key Insights

User Testing · Round 1

  • 4 participants with mixed experience using Sephora and beauty apps.
  • Tasks: find a gift, recall a perfume, compare moisturizers, check pickup.
  • People struggled most with comparing moisturizers and understanding fulfillment & size/price changes.

What We Heard

  • “Too much stuff on the screen… hard to read and hard to see.”
  • “I didn’t find a compare feature so I had to go back and forth.”
  • Users relied heavily on badges & reviews but wanted clearer summaries.

Competitive Analysis

  • Nike & Fabletics: strong hierarchy, sticky headers, clear CTAs.
  • Ulta: more whitespace, bold fulfillment tiles, clearer shade + price.
  • Takeaway: visual breathing room + consistent sections matter more than adding flashy features.

Redesign Highlights

1. Clear, Anchored Layout

We introduced a sticky top bar with product name/brand and a sticky “Add to Bag” button at the bottom. No matter how far users scroll, they always know what they’re looking at and how to act.

2. Full-Width Hero Image Carousel

A large, zoomable image gallery gives shoppers an immediate visual feel for the product. A peek of the next image hints that they can swipe, reducing reliance on tiny pagination dots.

3. Simpler Shipping & Pickup

We replaced dense text tiles with straightforward radio options like “Ship to address” and “Same-day pickup,” plus a single tile that updates with the current store / address. Extra details only appear after selection, lowering cognitive load.

4. Light-weight Drawers for Details

Long, text-heavy sections became short dropdown drawers (e.g., Details, Ingredients, How to use). Each is scannable in under ~100 words, so users don’t have to wade through walls of text to understand the product.

5. Highlights & Recommendations Carousels

A concise “Highlights” row surfaces skin type, texture, and key benefits at a glance, while “We think you’ll love” gently suggests similar products in a horizontal carousel instead of long grids.

Impact

  • In Round 2 testing, users described Redesign A as “clean,” “usable,” and “easy to navigate” and reached key info (skin type, ingredients, pickup) more quickly.
  • The Highlights + drawers pattern became the primary way people checked if the moisturizer fit Rosalie’s skin needs.
  • The sticky Add to Bag with clear “Item added successfully” state reduced uncertainty around checkout actions.

What I’d Do Next

  • Add a lighter, earlier comparison module using the stronger design (A).
  • Bring in a more prominent “clean” label directly on the hero image.
  • Explore adaptive layouts for newer vs. expert shoppers (e.g., quick summaries vs. deeper ingredient breakdowns).

Sephora PDP Redesign

Role UX / UI Design, User Research
Duration 4 Weeks · 2025
Tools Figma, Balsamiq, Notion

Context

Sephora’s mobile product details page feels dense and overwhelming, especially for newer skincare shoppers. Important information like skin concerns, ingredients, and pickup options are buried in long blocks of text, making it hard to know whether a product is a good fit.



Working with a team of three, I led the UX design and research for a redesign focused on clarity and confidence: helping people quickly understand if a product matches their needs and making it easier to compare options without feeling lost.

The Problem

Sephora’s product page overwhelmed shoppers with dense text and competing sections. It was hard to quickly tell if a product matched their skin needs, and comparing two moisturizers meant jumping back and forth between pages while trying to remember details.

The Solution

We redesigned the PDP to be calmer and more structured: clear highlights for skin type and concerns, simplified shipping & pickup options, and short expandable sections for details and ingredients. This lets shoppers scan quickly, compare confidently, and add to bag without second-guessing.







Design

Lo-Fi Prototypes

We started with quick, hand-drawn wireframes to explore different ways to organize product information, highlights, and fulfillment options. These sketches helped us test layout ideas early without worrying about typography or visual details.







Design

Hi-Fi Prototypes

Once the structure tested well, we moved into hi-fi screens that applied Sephora’s brand, refined hierarchy, and interactive states. We focused on creating a calmer visual rhythm, clearer CTA moments, and accessible type and contrast.



Redesign Highlights

  • Calmer layout: clearer sections, more whitespace, and a sticky “Add to Bag” button.


  • At-a-glance highlights: chips for skin type, concerns, and texture above the fold.


  • Simplified fulfillment: radio-style options for shipping vs. pickup with a single, clear summary.


  • Expandable drawers: short, scannable sections for details, ingredients, and how-to-use.


Final Takeaways

This project reminded me that good ecommerce design isn’t about adding more features—it’s about reducing friction and supporting confident decisions.

Clarity beats complexity

The biggest wins came from simplifying layout and copy, not from flashy new components. Clear hierarchy and short summaries made the PDP feel instantly more usable.

Surface what shoppers actually scan

Users relied most on highlights, badges, and a quick sense of fit (skin type, concerns, price). Designing around those scan patterns made comparison and decision-making much easier.

Test early, test often

Round 1 testing exposed issues we didn’t notice in Figma. Iterating with real tasks and real users helped us justify design choices and refine the final direction with more confidence.
